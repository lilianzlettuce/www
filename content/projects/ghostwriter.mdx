      An ancient writing machine rewired to move with a mind of its own... or, just an automated typewriter.
      
      Ghostwriter is a project about hidden labor, artificial recreation, and authenticity in the age of digital recreation. 
      The repurposed typewriter is made to print out text excerpts including ascii recreations of my artwork, and meaningless lipogrammatic poems limitted by the shape of its own keyboard.
      It is an ode to obsolete technologies, devices rendered useless by their more progressive counterparts, as well as an examination of language and mediated communication.
      The audience watches with glee as the machine drudges on, forced to endlessly repeat a manifesto it had no part in writing.

      It was ultimately exhibited at <a href="https://spill.purduehackers.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">spill</a>, Purdue Hackers’ 2025 end-of-year showcase.

    #### Context

    In fall of 2025 I acquired a electric typewriter for $10 from Facebook Marketplace. It was nearly fully functional— just a few stuck keys and an occasional ugly grinding sound emitted by the motor. 
    Naturally the next step was to take it apart and make it do something it was entirely not meant to do.
    What follows is the two-month saga of me learning the hard way that I am not a mechanical engineer.

    I cycled through many ideas, before, during, and after its creation. The initial concept was automating the machine to be interactive through voice transcription. The typewriter would be programmed to output a specific text/ascii image, which viewers will have the power to interrupt. 
    Upon acquiring a second typewriter off of Facebook Marketplace, I then brainstormed ideas involving networked typewriters capable of communication over Wifi.
    I spent some time researching the history the typewriter, drawing both on its role in giving women more freedom (as a medium for communication outside of the patriarchal sphere of influence), and a medium for making a unique form of art.
    Making modern connections to technological influences, data, censorship, the age of misinformation.

    Ultimately due to time constraints I settled on a much simpler goal. Perhaps in its simplicity the message is made clearer... but I may also go back to those earlier ideas in a later iteration.

    ##### Mechanical Design

    Opened up the typewriter to see the innards.
    How it works is that a motor spins really fast inside, which also makes a few other things (long metal rods) spin really fast, which powers every part of the machine in a really intricate interconnected way.
    Basically there's no smart circuitry at all inside this thing, which meant I couldn't send an electric signal to a key to make it move. Bummer.

    <Section className="w-full flex justify-end text-xs font-medium font-roboto-mono">

    But something else I noticed was that on the inside, all the key triggers (the long thin metal bits) are lined up neatly in a nice line.
    Moving a trigger up so that it hits the fast rotating metal rod causes a key to be activated. This motion could easily be replicated with a servor motor.
    But how would one put together something capable of accessing multiple keys? The obvious brute force solution was to use another servo motor for every key.
    But that would require at least 26 servo motors, neither space nor cost effective.

    Contraption will rest underneath typewriter
    Moves back and forth linearly to position itself under a specific key, then uses a servo arm to hit the metal bit that triggers that key

    Rack and pinion for linear motion
    - use modulus 1
    - small teeth for better precision
    - pinion gear stays in place => rotated by servo gear

    Goals (in order of priority):
    - Be able to automate typing with all normal keys 
    - Mechanism for triggering carriage return
    - Mechanism for space bar

    Necessary parts
    - 1-3 microservo motors (2.5kg) for triggering the keys (don't need much force at all)
    - 1 20kg servo motor for moving the microservos into the correct position
    - 3d printed rack and pinion parts
    - screws and nuts/bolts to assemble it?
