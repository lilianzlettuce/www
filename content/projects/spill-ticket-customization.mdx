  #### Overview

  A series of digital ticket designs and a corresponding customization experience I built for Spill, Purdue Hackers’ yearly showcase of projects from across the community.

  A lot of work went into organizing Spill—many of the organizers were also showcasing projects themselves—so this was a small contribution meant to support that effort while adding something playful and memorable to the event.

  The idea was simple: give people a personalized digital ticket they could keep, share, and use to spread the word. It also doubled as a small easter egg within the site.

  [image]

  #### Motivation

  This wasn’t solving a critical problem—it was more about amplifying the energy around the event.

  Make it easy to promote and share Spill
  Give people a personalizable digital token
  Add something fun and slightly unexpected to the site

  We had seen similar experiences for other events—especially the Socratica Symposium ticket customizer and some Next.js event card generators—and wanted to bring that same sense of personalization into this context.

  #### Visual Direction

  The ticket designs started from a loose concept: coffee shop packaging meets digital ticketing.

  We looked at a lot of café branding—paper textures, imperfect ink, handwritten elements—and combined that with structured, data-like visuals you’d expect on tickets: barcodes, mono text, pixel grids.

  The result leans into contrast:

  soft, sketchy, slightly messy visuals
  paired with rigid, encoded ticket details

  [image]

  This direction carried into the UI as well. Users choose from 8 ticket variations, framed as drink orders: matcha latte, cappuccino, etc.

  Two base “flavors”: tea (green) and coffee (brown)
  Each variation maps to a specific drink + visual style
  Subtle details like latte swirl patterns or pixel stamps reinforce the theme
  
  #### The Experience

  The customization flow was designed to feel lightweight and a bit playful, without getting in the way.

  After RSVPing, users are redirected straight into the ticket builder with their name and ticket number already filled in. From there, they can:

  Choose a ticket design (“drink order”)
  Edit their name / ticket number
  Add a short message on a sticky note
  Draw directly onto the canvas with a crayon-like tool
  Interact with the ticket via subtle 3D tilt on mouse movement

  [image]

  The background includes small, tactile elements—paper textures, coffee stains, teabags—that reinforce the physical feel of something that’s actually digital.

  #### Sharing + Circulation

  A big goal was making the ticket easy to pass around.

  Clicking “share” generates an image of the ticket and stores it in a Cloudflare R2 bucket. Each ticket gets its own page:

  Displays the customized ticket
  Includes a “join us at Spill” call-to-action
  Links back to RSVP + the main site

  [image]

  Users can:

  Copy a shareable link
  Download the image
  Use native device sharing
  Pre-fill a post on X/Twitter

  This turned each ticket into a small entry point back into the event—less like a static asset, more like a mini landing page.

  #### UX Decisions

  A surprising amount of thought went into how this connects to the rest of the site.

  How should people get here?

  Option A: separate page
  Option B: gated behind RSVP
  Option C: optional “customize ticket” button

  I landed on a hybrid:

  Users are automatically redirected after RSVP
  The page is still accessible later for editing/sharing

  This keeps the flow tight while not locking people out.

  What does “sharing” actually mean here?
  Instead of picking one format, I included multiple options (link, image, native share), since people share differently depending on context.

  #### Technical Notes

  The hardest part was exporting the ticket as an image reliably across devices.

  I explored a few approaches:

  canvas-based rendering (easy export, harder styling)
  DOM-based rendering (easy styling, harder export)

  I tried tools like fabric.js, konva.js, and html2canvas. Desktop worked fine, but mobile introduced issues—missing elements, incorrect fonts, layout shifts.

  I ultimately stuck with a DOM + html2canvas approach, with a workaround that exports only the ticket element instead of the full canvas.

  #### Physical Extension

  Alongside the digital tickets, we also made custom tea bags for attendees.

  Designed vector graphics for tags
  Used a Cricut to cut paper + gold foil
  Heat-pressed designs onto each tag
  Assembled tea bags by hand using loose-leaf tea

  [image]

  This tied back into the same “coffee/tea” theme and made the digital concept feel a bit more tangible at the event itself.

  #### Outcome

  This was a small project, but it ended up doing a few things well:

  Gave people something personal to share leading up to the event
  Helped keep Spill visible within the community
  Added a layer of interaction beyond a typical RSVP flow
  Created a consistent visual identity across digital and physical pieces

  It also sparked a lot of casual engagement—people comparing tickets, sharing designs, and treating them more like collectibles than just event confirmations.

  #### Reflection

  This project sits somewhere between design, dev, and community-building.

  It’s not a “product” in the usual sense, but it still benefits from the same kind of thinking—how something feels, how it flows, and how people actually engage with it.

  In this case, the goal wasn’t efficiency—it was creating a small moment of delight that people would want to pass along.
