  #### Context

  Every year, <a href="https://purduehackers.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Purdue Hackers</a> hosts a showcase of cool passion projects from across the community, ranging anywhere from hardware hacks to hand-sewed garments to algorithmic art. 
  The 2025 show <a href="https://spill.purduehackers.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">spill</a> took place within a small, cozy coffee shop at the edge of campus, with 23 projects exhibited and over 200 attendees showing up to the 3-hour event.

  #### Motivation

  As part of the spill task force, my goal was to make the event personable and welcoming to potential attendees by adding playful yet memorable touches that would really make the experience stand out.
  
  After brainstorming several ideas, we decided to give attendees personalized digital and physical tokens they could:
  - Save as a keepsake
  - Easily share with friends
  - Use to spread the word about the event
  
  With this goal in mind, I designed series of ticket designs and a corresponding customization experience as part of the RSVP flow of the site.
  We took inspiration from similar experiences for other events like the <a href="https://symposium.socratica.info/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Socratica Symposium</a>  ticket customizer, along with Nextjs event nametag cards.
  We wanted to bring that same sense of personalization into this context.

  #### Visual Direction

  The ticket designs started from the loose concept of combining coffee shop packaging with digital ticketing.
  We were inspired by many café branding paper textures with loose/flowy ink and messy handwritten elements. 
  
  Overall directions were this cozy coffee house vibe incorporated with digital data-encoding elements commonly seen on tickets (barcodes, pixel patterns, mono text strings). 
  The result leans in to contrast:
  - soft, sketchy, slightly messy visuals
  - paired with rigid, encoded ticket details

  This direction carried into the UI as well. Users choose from 8 ticket variations, framed as drink orders: matcha latte, cappuccino, etc.

  Two base “flavors”: tea (green) and coffee (brown)
  Each variation maps to a specific drink + visual style
  Subtle details like latte swirl patterns or pixel stamps reinforce the theme

  The customization flow was designed to feel lightweight and fun without getting in the way.

  After RSVPing, users were redirected straight into the ticket builder with their name and ticket number automatically filled in.

  #### Sharing + Circulation

  A big goal was making the ticket easy to pass around.

  Clicking “share” generates an image of the HTML canvas and stores it in a Cloudflare R2 bucket. Each ticket gets its own page which:
  - Displays the customized ticket
  - Includes a “make your own” call-to-action
  - Links back to RSVP + the main site

  Users can:

  - Copy a shareable link
  - Download the image
  - Use native device sharing
  - Pre-fill a post on X/Twitter

  This turned each ticket into a small entry point back into the event, encouraging more interested viewers to sign up.

  #### UX Decisions

  A surprising amount of thought went into how this connects to the rest of the site.

  How should people get here?

  - Option A: separate page
  - Option B: gated behind RSVP
  - Option C: optional “customize ticket” button

  We landed on a hybrid where users are automatically redirected to a separate ticket page after RSVP-ing, but the page is still accessible later for editing/sharing. 
  This keeps the flow tight while not locking people out.

  What does “sharing” actually mean here?

  Instead of picking one format, we included multiple options (link, image, native share), since people share differently depending on context.

  #### Technical Notes

  The most challenging part in developing the site was exporting the ticket canvas as an image reliably across devices.
  There were basically 2 approaches:

  - canvas-based rendering (easy export, harder styling)
  - DOM-based rendering (easy styling, harder export)

  I tried tools like fabric.js, konva.js, and html2canvas. Desktop worked fine, but mobile introduced issues like missing elements, incorrect fonts, and layout shifts.
  I ultimately stuck with a DOM + html2canvas approach, with a mobile workaround that exports only the ticket element instead of the full canvas.

  #### Physical Extension

  Alongside the digital tickets, we also made custom tea bags for attendees.

  - Designed vector graphics for tags
  - Used a Cricut to cut paper + gold foil
  - Heat-pressed designs onto each tag
  - Assembled tea bags by hand using loose-leaf tea

  This tied back into the same “coffee/tea” theme and made the digital concept feel a bit more tangible.

  #### Outcome

  Although this was a relatively small project, it ended up doing a few things well:

  - Gave people something personal to share leading up to the event
  - Helped keep spill visible within the community
  - Added a layer of interaction beyond a typical RSVP flow
  - Created a consistent visual identity across digital and physical pieces

  It also sparked a lot of casual engagement—people comparing tickets, sharing designs, and treating them more like collectibles than just event confirmations.
  Although it’s not a typical product, it still benefited from the same kind of thinking about how something feels and how people actually engage with it.
